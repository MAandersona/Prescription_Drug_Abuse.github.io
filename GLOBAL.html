Global Perspecties on prescription drug abuse

Global Perspective

Prescription drug abuse refers to the use of prescription medications in a way that is not intended or recommended by the prescribing healthcare provider. This can include taking someone else's prescription medication, taking a medication in a different way or at a higher dose than prescribed, or using a prescription medication for the wrong reason, such as to get high. (Mayo clinic, 2022).

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), the non-medical use of prescription drugs is a significant public health concern in many countries. In a report published in 2014, the WHO estimates that the non-medical use of prescription drugs accounts for between 3% and 11% of the global burden of disease and injury. WHO, (2014).

The prevalence of prescription drug abuse varies widely by country and it may be difficult to accurately estimate due to variations in definitions and reporting. However, the non-medical use of prescription drugs is a significant public health concern in many countries.

In comparison to other forms of drug abuse, the dangers of prescription drug abuse can vary. For example, the abuse of some prescription drugs, such as opioids, can be particularly dangerous due to their high potential for overdose and addiction. However, the abuse of other drugs, such as stimulants or hallucinogens, can also have serious consequences. It is important to note that all forms of drug abuse carry risks and can have negative impacts on an individual's health and well-being.

A study published in the Journal of Addiction Medicine in 2015 found that the prevalence of prescription drug abuse may be underestimated in the general population due to underreporting in surveys and other forms of self-report data. The authors of the study suggest that alternative methods, such as drug testing, may be needed to accurately assess the prevalence of prescription drug abuse in the general population. Klaine, E., Kranzler, H. R., & Calvert, G. M. (2015). Prescription drug abuse prevalence: Do self-report data underestimate the problem? Journal of Addiction Medicine, 9(6), 431-436. The prevalence of prescription drug abuse may also vary depending on the specific population being studied.

There are a number of measures that have been implemented to prevent prescription drug abuse. These measures may include:

  • Education and awareness campaigns: These campaigns aim to educate the public about the risks of prescription drug abuse and how to properly use and dispose of prescription drugs. These campaigns may be targeted at specific populations, such as teenagers or individuals with chronic pain.
  • Disposal programs: These programs provide safe and convenient ways for individuals to dispose of unused or expired prescription drugs, which can help prevent the misuse of prescription drugs.
  • Etc

It is possible that some members of the public may be unaware of the risks and consequences of prescription drug abuse due to misconceptions about the safety of prescription drugs, limited education about prescription drug abuse, the misuse of prescription drugs in a social context, and difficulty recognizing the signs of prescription drug abuse.

Can prescriprion drugs cause drug addiction?

Prescription drugs cause addiction quite easily, and most prescription drug addictions start with the misuse of a prescription that is meant for short-term as-needed use. Opioids are one of the most commonly abused prescription drugs, and these drugs produce a euphoric effect. The symptoms of misuse include euphoria, lethargy, drowsiness, confusion, dizziness, headaches, seizures, nausea, and difficulty breathing. Some of the commonly abused pain medications include hydrocodone, oxycodone, codeine, fentanyl, and meperidine. (https://newmaninterventions.com)

What are the effects of prescription drug abuse?

The effects of prescription drugs are dangerous and result in thousands of overdose deaths every year. Most prescription drug overdose deaths are unintentional and involve the combination of more than one drug. The misuse of prescription drugs leads to tolerance for the effects of drugs, causing dependency, which leads to developing drug-seeking behavior or addiction. There are numerous effects of prescription drug abuse, and these are both long-term, and short-term effects. Most prescription drug users will develop social isolation, and when they become addicted to the drugs they are using, they will often use these drugs alone. The long-term use of prescription drugs has the potential to result in job loss or even lead to homelessness. Prescription drug users will have increasing financial troubles, legal problems, and struggle with the consequences of risk-taking behavior.(https://newmaninterventions.com)

Can you be addicted to prescription drugs prescribed by a doctor?

Yes, you can become addicted to prescription drugs prescribed to you by a prescribing doctor. The most common prescription drugs people become addicted to are prescription opioids, central nervous system depressants, central nervous system stimulants, and prescription sedatives. There is a dangerous risk of addiction and severe health consequences if these drugs are not taken as directed by a health care professional. Prescription pain reliever misuse was the second most common form of illicit drug use in the United States in 2018. It was estimated that around 3.6% of the population was misusing pain medication is some way. In 2017 an estimated 18 million people in the United States were abusing prescription medication. Despite these drugs being prescribed for medical purposes, millions of Americans become addicted to them. The high prevalence of prescription drug misuse varies depending on age, gender, and other factors. The ease of access to these drugs is the leading cause of why someone becomes addicted to them.(https://newmaninterventions.com)

Are prescription drugs safe?

Prescription drugs are safe to use, but in the way as recommended by healthcare provider and in the desired dose. If the purpose or dose is varied, it could lead to lethal effects. (https://www.medindia.net)

Are there symptoms of withdrawal from prescription drugs?

Yes, some of the symptoms include restlessness, irritability, decreased self-confidence, tremors, sweating and insomnia. (https://www.medindia.net)

How can prescription drug misuse be prevented?

Preventing or stopping nonmedical use of prescription drugs is an important part of patient care. However, certain patients can benefit from prescription stimulants, sedatives, or opioid pain relievers. Therefore, physicians should balance the legitimate medical needs of patients with the potential risk for misuse and related harms. Patients can take steps to ensure that they use prescription medications appropriately by; following the directions as explained on the label or by the pharmacist, never stopping or changing a dosing regimen without first discussing it with the doctor, never using another person's prescription and never giving their prescription medications to others. More research can also be done inventing effective, non-addicting medications. (https://nida.nih.gov/)

Is There Treatment for Prescription Drug Addiction?

Treatment for opioid addiction includes medications that can help people get control without a high chance of addiction. Buprenorphine treats opiate withdrawal and dependence. Doctors often use it along with the drug naloxone (a combination that can be called Bunavail, Suboxone, or Zubsolv) to prevent relapse. If you've been taking buprenorphine in pill form and your body has gotten rid of all of the drug you were abusing, you might have another form of buprenorphine implanted under your skin. This is called Probuphine. It provides a constant dose of buprenorphine for 6 months. Buprenorphine also comes as a monthly shot called Sublocade. Other drug treatments for opiate withdrawal include methadone and the blood pressure medicine clonidine. Naltrexone blocks the effects of opiates and can prevent a relapse. It can be taken orally (Revia) or as a monthly injection (Vivitrol). Doctors recommend that people who misuse opioids keep naloxone, a medication that can reverse an overdose. It comes in a shot (Evzio) and a nasal spray (Narcan). Experts believe that “medication-assisted treatment” with methadone, naltrexone, or suboxone and cognitive behavioral therapy is the best treatment for most patients who have an opioid addiction. Counseling is the most common treatment for addiction to CNS depressants or stimulants. You might also need to detoxify (“detox”) your body under a doctor's care. (https://www.webmd.com)
